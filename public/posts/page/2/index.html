Modern Orientalism:

A budding idea in progress

Weebs.

Yogi influencers popping up everywhere.

I myself am perilously entangled at the intersection of the „East/West“ collide as someone who is obviously of Eastern descent, yet was raised entirely in the West. I’m „2nd gen“, meaning I was the second generation born in „The New World“; my grandparents were the last generation raised in „The Old World“.

Growing up, kids and adults alike often treated me differently. Subtly, such as when I was gifted pretty chopsticks, or when everyone looked expectantly at me on Multicultural Day, and less subtly when guys said they preferred Asian women and tried pick-up lines on me in Vietnamese or Japanese. I am neither of those ethnicities, and arguably I’m not even the ethnicity I was born into; when I went back to the Homeland I was immediately identified as an outsider and targeted by scam artists.

Why do I blog?

Isn’t keeping a blog a bit… egocentric?

I mean it could be but it doesn’t have to be. What matters is the purpose. For instance, I blog as a means to reclaim my online experience. I don’t post so much on social media because I have this space that’s completely mine- no tracking, to ads, no weird internet politics. I built this thing myself so I know exactly what’s going on behind the scenes. You can’t say that about any social media site or really anything else if you didn’t build it yourself.

Hot Takes : Pre-30 edition

Pets shouldn’t be treated like people.

They can still be treated respectfully, as pets, but worrying about hurting their feelings by not including them at the table, for example, is going too far. „This is my baby!“ No, that’s your dog. „She’s family; don’t exclude her.“ No, that’s a cat on the table with paws that were just scratching poop in a box. Why do people need to coddle their pets? Why not spend that energy trying to spoil your human friends?

Let’s Host a Death Fest

Let’s put the “fun” in funeral, baby.

It’s helpful to have an intentional outlet to interact and examine something as profound as death. Even better to make it a collaborative process.

Make it a party. Or maybe a cozy potluck. (This article is still being heavily edited while I figure out what I want/need to do.)

What is it all about?

It’s about being able to picture your own corpse and feel some sense of warmth or peace. It’s about being able to cordially look death in the eyes before your time comes, and to be at peace when you realize death is always in the room with you.
